Spatial Disparity Between Scenic Potential and Road Accessibility 
in Northern Pakistan: A Tehsil-Level Spatial Analysis

PROBLEM STATEMENT

Northern Pakistan (Gilgit-Baltistan and Upper Khyber Pakhtunkhwa) contains 
globally significant mountain landscapes but exhibits uneven tourism development. 
Infrastructure is concentrated along major corridors (e.g., Karakoram Highway), 
while potentially high-scenic regions remain remote and underdeveloped. Despite 
tourism's economic importance, no quantitative, spatially explicit framework 
systematically identifies disparities between scenic potential and accessibility 
at the Tehsil (sub-district) scale.

RESEARCH QUESTIONS

1. Where are statistically significant clusters and spatial outliers of scenic 
   potential located?
2. Which Tehsils represent "High Scenic–Low Accessibility" priority regions 
   for targeted development?
3. Does accessibility meaningfully predict scenic potential variation across 
   the region?

SIGNIFICANCE: Evidence-based answers support sustainable tourism planning, 
conservation prioritization, and balanced regional development policy.

METHODOLOGY

STUDY DESIGN: Spatial analysis of 60-80 Tehsils using composite indices and 
local spatial clustering methods.

SCENIC POTENTIAL INDEX (SPI):
Composite indicator combining z-score normalized components:
- Terrain Ruggedness Index (SRTM 30m DEM; 40% weight): Topographic complexity
- Forest cover (ESA WorldCover 2021; 25% weight): Vegetation character
- Water bodies (ESA WorldCover; 20% weight): Hydrological features
- Snow/Ice coverage (MODIS/Sentinel-2; 15% weight): Glacier/peak aesthetics

Formula: SPI = 0.4×Z(TRI) + 0.25×Z(Forest) + 0.2×Z(Water) + 0.15×Z(Snow)

ACCESSIBILITY INDEX (AI):
AI = 0.5×Z(Road Density) - 0.5×Z(Distance to Roads)

Components derived from OpenStreetMap road network (primary/secondary roads only).

DATA SOURCES: All free and open-access
- Administrative boundaries: GeoBoundaries ADM3 (Pakistan, 554 units)
- Elevation: SRTM DEM v3.0 (USGS)
- Land cover: ESA WorldCover 2021
- Road network: OpenStreetMap via OSMnx
- Snow extent: MODIS or Sentinel-2

SPATIAL ANALYSIS TECHNIQUES:
- Queen contiguity weights matrix (row-standardized)
- Global Moran's I test (spatial autocorrelation)
- Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) for cluster identification 
  (HH, LL, HL, LH classifications)
- Ordinary Least Squares regression: SPI ~ AI
- Conditional spatial lag model (if n > 30 and residual autocorrelation detected)

IMPLEMENTATION: Python with open-source libraries (geopandas, rasterio, 
libpysal, esda, scikit-learn). Reproducible Jupyter notebook workflow with 
persistent caching of external data.

OBJECTIVES

1. Develop reproducible, open-source framework for measuring scenic potential 
   and accessibility in mountain regions.
2. Identify statistically significant high-scenic/low-accessibility Tehsils 
   (realistic expectation: 5-15 units among 60-80).
3. Quantify spatial association between accessibility and scenic potential; 
   evaluate causal mechanisms.
4. Provide evidence-based guidance for sustainable tourism and conservation 
   planning.

EXPECTED OUTCOMES

1. Tehsil-level SPI and AI datasets with geospatial visualization
2. LISA cluster maps identifying scenic concentration areas and spatial outliers
3. Regression model quantifying accessibility-scenery association
4. Priority region identification for development feasibility assessment
5. Fully reproducible Python workflow applicable to other mountain regions
6. Technical report (5-10 pages) with findings and policy recommendations

PROOF OF CONCEPT: Pilot study (March 2026) on Gilgit region validated all 
methodological components. Results: strong local LISA clustering (2 HH clusters, 
7 LL clusters), strong accessibility-scenery association (R² ≈ 0.85, small-sample 
estimate), and identification of priority regions (e.g., Shigar). Workflow fully 
reproducible.
