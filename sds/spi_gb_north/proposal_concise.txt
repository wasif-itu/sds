Spatial Disparity Between Scenic Potential and Road Accessibility in Northern Pakistan 497 words
Gilgit-Baltistan & Upper Khyber Pakhtunkhwa  ·  Open-source Python  ·  13–17 days

Problem
Northern Pakistan's mountain landscapes contain globally significant scenic resources yet lack systematic spatial analysis linking terrain character to infrastructure distribution. Road networks concentrate along accessible corridors such as the Karakoram Highway, creating measurable mismatches between scenic potential and transport access. These mismatches have direct consequences for tourism investment prioritization, conservation zoning, and regional development equity. No tehsil-scale evidence base currently exists to guide policy in this gap.

Research questions
Three questions frame the study: (1) Where do statistically significant scenic potential clusters and accessibility outliers occur at tehsil scale? (2) Which tehsils exhibit high-scenic, low-accessibility profiles as identified through LISA spatial outlier analysis? (3) To what extent does spatial accessibility predict scenic variation after controlling for spatial autocorrelation in residuals?

Methodology
Scenic Potential Index (SPI) operationalizes visual landscape quality through four biophysical components validated in mountain landscape preference literature — terrain relief, forest cover, surface water, and snow/ice extent — each standardized and combined with equal weights as the absence of Pakistan-specific preference surveys precludes empirical weighting:

SPI = 0.25·Z(TPI) + 0.25·Z(Forest%) + 0.25·Z(Water%) + 0.25·Z(Snow/Ice%)
Topographic Position Index uses a 300m focal mean on SRTM 30m DEM with landform classification following Weiss (2001). Terrain ruggedness is computed per Riley et al. (1999). Forest and water cover derive from ESA WorldCover (10m); snow/ice from MODIS MOD10A2 (500m native, treated as binary presence at tehsil level via zonal statistics — resolution mismatch is acknowledged as a limitation). All layers are reprojected to EPSG:32643 at 100m.

Accessibility Index replaces Euclidean distance with least-cost path distance over a slope-derived friction surface from the same DEM, addressing the fundamental terrain-barrier problem that flat-space distance measures cannot capture in this landscape:

AI = 0.5·Z(Road Density) − 0.5·Z(Cost-Distance to Roads)
OSM primary and secondary roads (OSMnx) provide the road network. Coverage gaps in remote tehsils are treated as lower bounds on inaccessibility, not precise measurements. Spatial analysis proceeds as: Global Moran's I for SPI and AI independently; LISA (Anselin 1995) with queen contiguity weights for cluster and outlier mapping (HH/LL clusters, HL/LH outliers); OLS regression (SPI ~ AI) with Moran's I residual diagnostic; Lagrange Multiplier tests to select between spatial lag and spatial error specifications (Anselin 1988); final coefficient estimates from the spatially-corrected model. Sensitivity analysis perturbs SPI weights by ±0.15 to test index stability. Study area covers 60–80 tehsils across Gilgit-Baltistan and upper KP. Findings will identify priority regions for evidence-based infrastructure investment while acknowledging that spatial opportunity analysis does not substitute for participatory planning with affected communities.

Deliverables
Reproducible Jupyter notebooks; choropleth maps of SPI, AI, and LISA clusters; spatial regression output tables; GeoJSON data for GIS integration; technical report with policy recommendations.
