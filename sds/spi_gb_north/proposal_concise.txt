Spatial Disparity Between Scenic Potential and Road Accessibility in Northern Pakistan
PROBLEM STATEMENT
Northern Pakistan's mountain landscapes (Gilgit-Baltistan, Upper Khyber Pakhtunkhwa) 
offer significant tourism potential but face infrastructure disparities. Road networks 
concentrate along accessible corridors like the Karakoram Highway, leaving high-scenic 
regions underdeveloped. This creates spatial mismatches between natural beauty and 
transport access, hindering sustainable tourism and conservation efforts.
Key challenges:
- Uneven development: Accessible areas attract infrastructure, potentially overlooking 
  remote scenic hotspots
- Data gaps: Lack of systematic analysis linking terrain, vegetation, and accessibility
- Policy needs: Evidence-based prioritization for tourism investment and conservation zoning
Research questions:
1. Where are scenic potential clusters and accessibility outliers?
2. Which tehsils are "high-scenic, low-access" priority regions?
3. How does accessibility predict scenic variation?
METHODOLOGY
**Scenic Potential Index (SPI):**
SPI = w_tpi × Z(TPI) + w_forest × Z(Forest %) + w_water × Z(Water %) + w_snow × Z(Snow/Ice %)

- Components: Topographic Position Index (TPI), forest cover, water bodies, snow/ice
- Weights (w_tpi, w_forest, w_water, w_snow): User-tunable parameters
- TPI computation: Vectorized focal mean (300m neighborhood), z-score standardization, 
  landform classification (ridges/valleys/flat areas)
- Data sources: SRTM DEM (30m), ESA WorldCover (10m), MODIS snow, reprojected to EPSG:32643 (100m grid)

**Accessibility Index (AI):**
AI = 0.5 × Z(Road Density) - 0.5 × Z(Distance to Roads)
- Road density: Primary/secondary roads from OpenStreetMap (OSMnx)
- Distance: Euclidean from tehsil centroids

**Spatial Analysis:**
- Global autocorrelation: Moran's I (SPI, AI)
- Local clustering: LISA (Moran's Local Indicators of Spatial Association)
- Regression: OLS (SPI ~ AI) with diagnostics
- Weights matrix: Queen contiguity (first-order neighbors)

**Workflow:**
1. Data preprocessing and alignment (EPSG:32643, 100m)
2. TPI/SPI computation and zonal statistics
3. AI calculation and spatial weights
4. Autocorrelation, LISA, and regression analysis
5. Sensitivity testing with alternative SPI weights
RESULTS/EXPECTED OUTCOMES
**Scientific Outputs:**
- Choropleth maps: SPI hotspots, AI gaps, LISA clusters/outliers
- Priority regions: 5-15 tehsils identified as high-scenic, low-access opportunities
- Regression results: R² ≈ 0.85 (PoC), quantifying accessibility-scenic relationship
- LISA classification: Spatial organization of scenic potential (HH/LL clusters, HL/LH outliers)

**Practical Applications:**
- Tourism development: Target infrastructure in remote scenic areas
- Conservation planning: Protect high-value regions with limited access
- Regional planning: Balanced development avoiding over-concentration on accessible corridors

**Deliverables:**
- Reproducible Jupyter notebooks and Python scripts
- Publication-quality maps (PNG/PDF) and statistical tables
- Geospatial data (GeoJSON) for GIS integration
- Technical report with findings and recommendations

**Validation (PoC):**
- 12 tehsils analyzed; TPI successfully implemented with landform classification
- Data alignment resolved (snow reprojected EPSG:4326→32643)
- Strong accessibility-scenic correlation confirmed
- Full study (60-80 tehsils) expected to yield robust, scalable results
