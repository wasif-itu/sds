1. ABSTRACT
================================================================================

Northern Pakistan, including Gilgit-Baltistan and Upper Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, 
contains some of the most important mountain landscapes in the country. These 
areas play a major role in tourism and local economic activity. However, 
infrastructure development is often concentrated along historically accessible 
corridors such as the Karakoram Highway, which may leave other high-potential 
regions underdeveloped. 

This project identifies and analyzes spatial disparities between natural 
scenic potential and transportation accessibility at the Tehsil (ADM3) level, 
with particular focus on geographic clustering of scenic resources and 
accessibility "gaps"—areas with high scenic value but limited road access that 
represent priority targets for sustainable development and conservation planning.

A comprehensive proof-of-concept (PoC) study was conducted on the Gilgit region 
(12 complete tehsils), validating the methodology, resolving critical data 
alignment issues, and successfully implementing the Topographic Position Index 
(TPI) as a replacement for Terrain Ruggedness Index (TRI). The PoC demonstrated 
feasibility for full-scale implementation across Pakistan's northern administrative 
divisions, with all data layers properly aligned to a consistent spatial reference 
system.

4. LITERATURE FOUNDATION
================================================================================

This study builds on established frameworks:

SCENIC POTENTIAL & LANDSCAPE ASSESSMENT:
- Paracchini et al. (2014) demonstrate how measurable environmental indicators 
  can be combined to map recreation potential at large spatial scales.
- Landscape assessment literature (Lothian 1999, Swaffield and McShane 2013) 
  distinguishes between subjective perception and objective landscape features, 
  supporting the use of quantifiable natural attributes in spatial modeling.
- Environmental indices (forest cover, water availability, terrain diversity) 
  are established proxies for landscape aesthetic value.
- Weiss (2001) and Wilson et al. (2007) provide foundation for Topographic 
  Position Index (TPI) as an enhanced measure of terrain position relative to 
  neighborhood, offering more interpretable landform classification than TRI.

SPATIAL DEPENDENCY & CLUSTERING:
- Anselin (1995) introduces Local Indicators of Spatial Association (LISA) for 
  identifying clusters and spatial outliers—directly applicable to finding 
  high-scenic regions adjacent to accessible corridors.
- Queen contiguity weights are standard in regional geographic analysis.
- Moran's I tests global spatial autocorrelation; negative values (as found in 
  PoC) indicate dispersed patterns, suggesting outliers are more informative 
  than regional clusters.

ACCESSIBILITY & DEVELOPMENT:
- Transport accessibility is a primary driver of regional economic development 
  (Hanson and Guiliano 2004). In mountain regions, road networks are the binding 
  constraint.
- OSM road data has been used in accessibility studies across South Asia and provides 
  primary/secondary road filtering capability suitable for infrastructure-focused 
  accessibility analysis.

PROOF OF CONCEPT VALIDATION:
- PoC (conducted April 2026) confirmed all methodological components are 
  implementable with open-source tools and freely available data.
- Critical data alignment issue resolved: Snow data reprojected from EPSG:4326 
  to EPSG:32643 at 100m resolution to match study grid.
- TPI successfully implemented as TRI replacement: Vectorized computation with 
  300m neighborhood, z-score standardization, and landform classification.
- Results showed local spatial clustering of scenic potential (LISA: 7 low-low 
  clusters, 2 high-high clusters; p < 0.10 for 3 of 5 outliers).
- Accessibility Index strongly predicts scenic potential variation (R² = 0.8548, 
  OLS regression), supporting use as co-explanatory variable.
- TPI outputs validated: Ridge/valley classification captures mountain terrain 
  patterns effectively.

5. METHODOLOGY (UPDATED)
================================================================================

UNIT OF ANALYSIS:
- Primary: 60-80 Tehsils in Gilgit-Baltistan and Upper KP
- Secondary (if data permits): Full Pakistan ADM3 layer (554 total units, for 
  comparative context)
- Data quality filter: Analysis focuses on units with complete elevation and 
  land-cover data. Realistic expectation: 40-60 units with complete metrics.

5.1 SCENIC POTENTIAL INDEX (SPI) CONSTRUCTION
========================================

Formula (tunable weights, user-defined):
    SPI = w_tpi × Z(TPI) + w_forest × Z(Forest %) + w_water × Z(Water %) + w_snow × Z(Snow/Ice %)

Where Z(x) = (x - mean) / std_dev, all normalized to z-scores within study region.
Weights (w_tpi, w_forest, w_water, w_snow) are tunable parameters decided by the user 
based on landscape priorities and sensitivity analysis.

Component rationale:
- Topographic Position Index (TPI): User-defined weight (w_tpi)
  TPI measures elevation relative to neighborhood mean, providing interpretable 
  landform classification (ridges, valleys, flat areas). Calculated from SRTM 
  30m DEM using focal mean approach with 300m neighborhood radius, z-score 
  standardized for comparability.
  
- Forest cover: User-defined weight (w_forest)
  Forest presence indicates biodiversity, shade, water retention, and aesthetic 
  appeal. Extracted from ESA WorldCover 2021 (10m, resampled to 100m).
  
- Water bodies: User-defined weight (w_water)
  Rivers, lakes, and waterfalls are primary scenic attractions in mountains.
  Extracted from ESA WorldCover water class.
  
- Snow/Ice coverage: User-defined weight (w_snow)
  Glaciers and permanent snow are iconic scenic features (e.g., Karakoram peaks).
  Mapped using MODIS seasonal data, reprojected to match study grid (EPSG:32643).

Data processing:
1. TPI computation: Vectorized focal mean using scipy.ndimage.convolve, 
   300m neighborhood, z-score standardization, landform classification.
2. Zonal statistics: Calculate mean TPI, % forest, % water, % snow per Tehsil.
3. Z-score normalization: Standardize all metrics to mean=0, std=1.
4. Weighted sum: Combine with specified weights per formula.
5. Output: SPI raster (100m) and Tehsil-level SPI values (vector).

Weight Configuration & Sensitivity:
Weights are user-defined parameters that can be tuned based on landscape priorities, 
expert judgment, or empirical analysis. Example configurations include:
- Terrain-emphasized: Higher w_tpi (e.g., 0.5) for ruggedness-focused analysis
- Vegetation-emphasized: Higher w_forest + w_water (e.g., 0.45 combined) for ecological focus
- Equal weighting: w_tpi = w_forest = w_water = w_snow = 0.25 for balanced approach

Sensitivity analysis will test robustness under alternative weight configurations 
to ensure priority region identification remains stable across different weighting schemes.

PoC validation: SPI computed successfully with TPI; mean = -0.07, std = 0.95, 
range = [-0.25, 3.49] across 60-unit pilot. Distribution matches expected pattern 
(Gilgit and Nagar high scenic potential). TPI provides clearer landform 
interpretation than TRI.

5.2 ACCESSIBILITY INDEX (AI) CONSTRUCTION
==========================================

Formula (finalized via PoC):
    AI = 0.5×Z(Road Density) - 0.5×Z(Distance to Roads)

Where road density = (total primary+secondary road length) / Tehsil area (km/km²).
Distance = Euclidean distance from Tehsil centroid to nearest road (km).

Rationale:
- High road density indicates developed transport infrastructure.
- Low distance to roads indicates proximity to travel corridors.
- Negative sign on distance: Inverts so higher AI = more accessible.
- Z-score normalization: Makes both components comparable.

Data source: OpenStreetMap (OSM), extracted via OSMnx library.
Road types included: Primary roads, secondary roads (exclude tertiary, residential, 
footpaths to maintain consistency with "major accessibility"). Road filtering justified 
to reflect strategic transport infrastructure rather than local circulation.

Centroid Distance Assumption:
The centroid-based distance metric provides a standardized, comparable measure of 
accessibility at the Tehsil scale. In mountainous terrain with dispersed settlements, 
this represents a representative rather than absolute proximity measure. Future work 
may employ population-weighted centroids to reflect actual settlement distribution, 
particularly for regions with significant intra-Tehsil spatial variation.

PoC validation: AI computed successfully; mean = 1.14, std = 1.82, range = 
[0.40, 4.98] across pilot. Strong positive correlation with true accessibility 
(east-west valleys highly accessible; northern mountains remote).

5.3 SPATIAL WEIGHT MATRIX & NEIGHBORHOOD STRUCTURE
===================================================

Weights matrix: Queen contiguity (first-order neighbors)
- Two Tehsils are neighbors if they share an edge or corner.
- Row-standardized: Each row sums to 1.

PoC results: 60-unit pilot generated Queen weights with average 4.83 neighbors 
per unit (range: 2–7 neighbors). Matrix is well-connected (no isolated units).

5.4 GLOBAL SPATIAL AUTOCORRELATION ANALYSIS
=============================================

Test: Moran's I (two-tailed, 999 permutations)

Interpretation:
- Moran's I > 0: Values are spatially clustered (neighbors similar).
- Moran's I < 0: Values are dispersed (neighbors dissimilar).
- p-value < 0.05: Significant autocorrelation (reject null of spatial randomness).

PoC results (Gilgit pilot, n=12):
- Moran's I (SPI):  I = -0.2147, p = 0.0960
  Weak negative autocorrelation. Scenic potential is NOT clustered.
  
- Moran's I (AI):   I = -0.2021, p = 0.0640
  Marginally significant negative signal. Accessibility shows weak dispersion.

Expected for full study (n=60-80):
- Larger sample likely to produce more stable p-values.
- Negative autocorrelation suggests spatial outliers will be more informative 
  than regional clusters (justifies LISA emphasis).

5.5 LOCAL SPATIAL CLUSTERING ANALYSIS (LISA)
==============================================

Test: Moran's Local Indicators of Spatial Association (Moran_Local, esda library)
- Classification: Each unit labeled as HH (high scenic, high neighbors), 
  LL (low scenic, low neighbors), HL or LH (outliers), or Not Significant.
- Significance threshold: p < 0.10 (standard for local tests with smaller sample).

PoC results (n=12):
- HH clusters (scenic hotspots): 2 units (Nagar-I region)
- LL clusters (low-scenic regions): 7 units (central/southern Gilgit)
- HL outliers (scenic isolated among low-scenic): 1 unit (high-interest target)
- LH outliers (low-scenic isolated among high-scenic): 2 units
- Not Significant: 0 units

Interpretation:
Clear spatial organization of scenic potential at local level. LL cluster 
dominance suggests contiguous low-scenic plains; HH cluster in Nagar indicates 
scenic concentration around specific geographic features. TPI-based SPI provides 
more nuanced terrain representation for clustering analysis.

Output: LISA classification map (choropleth by cluster type) + priority 
geographic targets from HL/LH outliers.

5.6 REGRESSION ANALYSIS
=======================

APPROACH (UPDATED):
Primary: Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression
Secondary: Spatial lag model (only if n > 30 and residual autocorrelation detected)

PRIMARY MODEL:
    SPI ~ AI
    
    Objective: Does accessibility explain scenic potential variation?
    
PoC result: R² = 0.8548, coefficient β_AI = 0.7314 (highly significant)
Interpretation: Accessibility shows strong association with scenic potential in pilot. 
Positive coefficient unexpected (contrary to hypothesis); suggests high-access areas 
tend to have higher scenic value in pilot. TPI integration provides more accurate 
terrain representation for regression.

CAVEAT - Small Sample Inflation Risk:
This R² is computed on a small pilot sample (n=12) using composite indices both 
standardized within the study region. The association may be inflated due to shared 
construction methodology and reduced sample size. Full-study regression with n=60+ units 
will provide more reliable effect estimates and improved generalizability.

CAUSAL INTERPRETATION (IMPORTANT):
The positive AI-SPI correlation may reflect:
(a) Selection bias: Roads built in scenic areas first (reverse causality)
(b) Complementarity: Scenic areas attract road development
(c) Confounding: Accessible areas have better preservation of scenic features

PoC finding suggests caution in interpreting causality. Full study should:
- Test for temporal precedence (did roads come before tourism?)
- Consider instrumental variables approach
- Document road construction dates if available

SPATIAL LAG MODEL (OPTIONAL IF n > 30):
    SPI ~ AI + W*SPI
    
    Where W*SPI = spatially weighted average of neighboring Tehsils' SPI values 
    (row-standardized Queen weights).
    
PoC finding: Could not compute due to small sample (n=12) and weights matrix 
singularity. Full study with n=60+ recommended if Moran's I shows significant 
spatial dependence in residuals.

9. LIMITATIONS & ASSUMPTIONS (UPDATED)
================================================================================

METHODOLOGICAL:
1. Scenic potential is multidimensional and partially subjective. This index 
   captures objective landscape features known to correlate with aesthetic appeal, 
   but does not include cultural heritage, visitor preferences, or wildlife value.
   
2. Accessibility measured as roads only; does not account for:
   - Road quality or travel time
   - Seasonal closure (winter snow blocks passes)
   - Economic cost of travel
   Future: Incorporate travel-time matrices from routing engines.
   
3. Static analysis: Single time snapshot. Scenery and infrastructure change 
   seasonally and over years. Recommend longitudinal study (multi-year) if 
   data becomes available.

DATA QUALITY:
4. SRTM 30m DEM has known voids in extremely steep areas or clouds. Snow cover 
   on DEM affects TPI calculation. Mitigation: Use Sentinel-1 SAR data for voids 
   if available. PoC successfully handled NoData regions with neighborhood-aware 
   averaging.
   
5. OSM road data may lag reality in remote regions (volunteer-contributed, 
   incomplete in poorly-mapped areas). Mitigation: Cross-check with high-resolution 
   satellite imagery for major discrepancies.
   
6. Seasonal snow extent varies dramatically. Single snapshot may not represent 
   year-round scenic value. Recommendation: Use multi-seasonal average if time 
   permits. PoC resolved spatial alignment issues for consistent integration.

SPATIAL:
7. Note: PoC found weak negative Moran's I (-0.2147, p=0.096), indicating dispersed 
   spatial pattern rather than strong clustering. High-scenic units are NOT necessarily 
   adjacent to other high-scenic units. Results emphasize outliers and localized clusters 
   rather than regional broad patterns. TPI provides more interpretable terrain 
   representation for spatial analysis.

8. Queen contiguity weights may not reflect True "neighborhood" for mountain 
   terrain (e.g., south-facing slopes may be more similar than geometrically 
   adjacent units). Sensitivity analysis with alternative weights recommended 
   if robustness is critical.

TECHNICAL:
9. Vectorized TPI computation requires careful NoData handling. PoC identified 
   and resolved NaN comparison bugs in focal mean calculations, ensuring robust 
   implementation.
